The Bamboo Leaf Green of Mount Emei, Sichuan

What is Zhuyeqing?

Zhuyeqing (竹叶青, "Bamboo Leaf Green") is a premium flat-shaped green tea grown on the mist-shrouded slopes of Mount Emei (峨眉山) in Sichuan Province, China. The tea takes its poetic name from the shape of its leaves — slender, flat, and tapered at both ends, resembling tender bamboo leaves.

Mount Emei — where clouds, Buddhism, and tea have intertwined for millennia

The tea was officially named in 1964 by Marshal Chen Yi (陈毅), China's then-Foreign Minister. While visiting a Buddhist temple on Mount Emei, a monk served him a cup of the local spring tea. Enchanted by its fresh fragrance and the graceful dancing of leaves in the glass, Chen Yi asked for its name. When told it had none, he said: "The leaves look just like bamboo — let's call it Zhuyeqing."

"竹叶青青不肯黄，枝条楚楚耐严霜。" — The bamboo stays green and refuses to turn yellow, its branches stand elegant through the harshest frost.

Why Zhuyeqing is Special

🏔️ Three Gifts from Mount Emei

  • Altitude: Grown at 800–1,500 meters above sea level, where clouds and fog envelop the tea gardens year-round. The diffused sunlight slows leaf growth, concentrating amino acids and creating a natural sweetness
  • Climate: Mount Emei has over 320 foggy days per year — more than almost anywhere else in China. The persistent moisture and cool temperatures produce exceptionally tender buds
  • Soil: Rich, acidic mountain soil nourished by millennia of decaying forest litter, providing deep mineral complexity to the tea

🌿 What Makes It Unique

  • Single bud only (独芽): The highest grades use only the unopened bud — no leaves at all. It takes roughly 40,000 hand-picked buds to make one kilogram of top-grade Zhuyeqing
  • Standing leaves: When brewed in a glass, the buds stand upright in the water, gently swaying like a bamboo grove — a mesmerizing visual experience unique to this tea (more on this below ↓)
  • Spring harvest: Like Longjing, the best Zhuyeqing is picked before Qingming (清明). The earliest spring buds carry the concentrated essence of winter dormancy
  • Clean, chestnut fragrance: Unlike the bean-fragrance of Longjing, Zhuyeqing has a distinctive chestnut-like roasted aroma (栗香) with a lingering sweetness in the throat

How to Brew Zhuyeqing

  1. Vessel: Use a tall, clear glass cup (玻璃杯). This is essential — half the pleasure is watching the buds dance and stand upright in the water
  2. Water temperature: 85°C (185°F). Slightly hotter than Longjing, to coax out Zhuyeqing's chestnut aroma. Never use boiling water
  3. Method: Pour water first (about 200ml), then gently drop in 3–4g of tea. This is the "上投法" (top-drop method), best for single-bud teas — it prevents scalding the delicate buds
  4. Wait: Watch the buds slowly sink, swirl, and eventually stand upright at the bottom — this takes about 2–3 minutes. The liquor turns a clear, pale jade green
  5. Infusions: Good for 3 steeps. The first steep is the most aromatic; the second has the fullest body; the third is gentle and sweet

The Standing Buds · 竖立

This is the hallmark of Zhuyeqing and what sets it apart from every other Chinese green tea.

When you drop the buds into hot water, they slowly sink, twirl, and then — one by one — stand perfectly upright at the bottom of the glass, like a miniature bamboo grove swaying in a spring breeze.

The phenomenon only happens with perfectly shaped, single-bud tea of the right density. If the buds float sideways or clump together, the tea is likely lower grade or past its prime.

"观茶如观竹" — Watching the tea is like watching bamboo.

About Mount Emei

Mount Emei (峨眉山) is one of China's Four Sacred Buddhist Mountains and a UNESCO World Heritage Site (dual: cultural and natural). Rising to 3,099 meters, it is the highest of the four sacred mountains and home to over 30 Buddhist temples dating back to the 1st century CE.

The Golden Summit (金顶) — Samantabhadra's statue rising above an ocean of clouds

The mountain's unique microclimate — perpetual mist, diffused sunlight, and rich biodiversity — makes it one of China's finest terroirs for tea cultivation. Buddhist monks have been growing tea here since the Tang Dynasty (618–907 CE), originally for meditation practice. The caffeine and L-theanine in tea helped monks stay alert yet calm during long meditation sessions.

Zhuyeqing vs Longjing

Both are elite Chinese flat green teas, but they come from very different worlds:

🎋 Zhuyeqing 🍃 Longjing
Origin Mount Emei, Sichuan West Lake, Hangzhou
Altitude 800–1,500m (high mountain) 100–300m (hillside)
Leaf shape Slender needle, tapered Flat, broad, sword-like
Raw material Single bud (独芽) One bud + one leaf
Aroma Chestnut, roasted (栗香) Bean, grassy (豆香)
Taste Sweet, clean, lingering Mellow, umami, nutty
In the glass Buds stand upright ↑ Leaves float & sink gently
Liquor color Pale jade green Yellow-green
Cultural vibe Buddhist mountain, contemplative West Lake elegance, literary

"Longjing is the scholar's tea — refined and poetic. Zhuyeqing is the monk's tea — quiet, pure, and transcendent."

Neither is "better" — they represent two different philosophies of Chinese green tea. Longjing celebrates the gentle landscape of Jiangnan; Zhuyeqing channels the spiritual grandeur of Sichuan's sacred mountains.

Chinese Tea: A Simple Guide

Chinese tea is categorized by how the leaves are processed, especially the level of oxidation (exposure to air). Here are the main types:

🟢 Green Tea
Zero oxidation. Fresh, grassy, light.
Longjing, Zhuyeqing, Biluochun
🟡 Yellow Tea
Slight oxidation. Mellow and sweet.
Junshan Yinzhen, Mengding Huangya
🔵 White Tea
Minimal processing. Delicate and sweet.
Baihaoyinzhen, Shoumei
🟠 Oolong Tea
Partial oxidation (10–70%). Complex, floral.
Tieguanyin, Da Hong Pao
🔴 Black Tea
Full oxidation ("Red Tea" in China). Bold, malty.
Keemun, Yunnan Gold
⚫ Dark Tea
Post-fermented & aged. Earthy and smooth.
Hei Zhuan (Hunan Brick Tea)
🟤 Pu'er
Aged dark tea from Yunnan. Deep and complex.
Pu'er Cake, Liu Bao
🌸 Flower Tea
Green tea scented with flowers. Fragrant and refreshing.
Jasmine Tea, Rose Tea

Zhuyeqing is a Green Tea — it's pan-fired immediately after picking to stop oxidation, preserving its fresh character, chestnut aroma, and bright jade-green color.
